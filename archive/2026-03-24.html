Desktop AI agents are no longer a research demo — they're shipping. The competitive pressure is accelerating quality and access at a pace that benefits everyone building on top of these models.

But speed without security is a liability. The LiteLLM supply-chain compromise is a sobering reminder that the AI toolchain is only as strong as its weakest dependency — and with thousands of teams relying on unified LLM proxies, a single poisoned package can cascade fast. On the business side, the capital flows tell a clear story: $75M for AI video editing (Mirage/Captions), $11M for screen-reading recall (Littlebird), and OpenAI dangling 17.5% guaranteed returns to private equity — the ecosystem is maturing from experimental to institutional. The tools layer continues to specialize, with Mozilla AI launching a Stack Overflow for coding agents and Xiaomi pushing MiMo models for robots and voice. We're watching the AI stack stratify in real time: foundation models at the bottom, specialized agents in the middle, and an increasingly security-conscious infrastructure holding it all together.
